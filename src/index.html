Cognitive
distortions
in programming

As developers, we often encounter problems that contradict our productivity. But we often ignore the big picture. Some of these problems are inconspicuous, while others are significant. Sometimes you can deal with them somehow, and sometimes, unfortunately, you can't.

Together, they form something like a closed loop, and this can lead to prolonged loss of productivity, bugs, and endless disappointment.

If we can minimize the impact of one or more of these factors, we can break the cycle and neutralize the remnants.

Cognitive bias

IKEA effect

You overestimate your own decisions, and, as a result, underestimate the decisions of others.

How does it manifest?

The cognitive bias is called that way because consumers often prefer to buy products they can assemble themselves rather than pre-assembled ones.

We tend to overvalue our own decisions and undervalue the decisions of others. If you've ever worked in a company that used a clunky internal tool instead of a more convenient off-the-shelf solution, you understand what I'm talking about.

Cognitive bias

Premature optimization

You optimize something long before the need for it arises.

How does it manifest?

If you add an aerodynamic spoiler to your old car instead of repairing the engine, the car won't become faster.

Great example: writing highly tuned and high-performance code for just an experimental project.

Cognitive bias

Distortion of novelty

You give higher importance to events that have happened recently and underestimate older ones.

How does it manifest?

This distortion often hits us when we need to solve a problem, and... oh! We've just solved a similar problem! Let's use the same solution because it worked, and we remember how to do it!

Do you notice that you're using the same working methods repeatedly? If so, perhaps you're looking at different problems from the same perspective.

Cognitive bias

Hyperbolic depreciation

You chase immediate small gains and ignore larger ones that are currently unavailable but attainable in the future.

How does it manifest?

Have you ever procrastinated writing a test? Caught yourself using arrow keys in Vim? Congratulations, you are experiencing hyperbolic discounting.

The immediate benefit of using something readily accessible significantly eases the pain of enduring to learn the correct syntax for moving to the desired line. But once you grasp how to navigate faster, the future benefits are much greater. In the end, you will save a lot of time.

Cognitive bias

Errors in planning

Incorrectly estimating the time needed to complete a task.

How does it manifest?

One of the biases that most of us should acknowledge. Both project managers and product users are prone to setting deadlines that are impossible to meet.

This is well expressed in the old aphorism:

The first 90% of the code takes the first 90% of the development time. The remaining 10% takes the second 90% of the development time.
